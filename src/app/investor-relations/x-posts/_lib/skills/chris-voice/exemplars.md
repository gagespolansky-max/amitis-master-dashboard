# Chris Solarz — Format Exemplars

Few-shot examples for the `chris-voice` skill. 27 exemplars across 8 structural formats, curated for diversity within each format.

The full taxonomy with skeleton breakdowns is below. For each draft, identify which format(s) suit the input and angle, then study the exemplars in that format. **Don't copy phrasing — extract the pattern.**

---

## 1. Punchy Declarative + Explainer

**When to use:** when a single strong claim should land first, then back it up. Most common shape in Chris's corpus. Used for thesis-style posts, market observations, "here's why this matters" statements.

**Skeleton:** (1) one short period-terminated declarative opener; (2) blank line; (3) explainer paragraph(s) with mechanism, supporting numbers, or "why" unpack; (4) optional forward-looking implication or one-liner kicker; (5) optional source link.

**Length:** 250–600 chars. **Source:** ~50/50 chart/link. **Signature moves:** opening sentence is a quotable standalone; heavy contrast pairs ("blessing/curse"); closes with allocator implication.

### Exemplar 1A — Treasury yields cut both ways for crypto (504c)
> Rising U.S. Treasury yields are both a blessing and a curse for crypto.
>
> The blessing: bond market volatility strengthens Bitcoin's appeal as a decentralized, non-sovereign asset with a fixed supply. The US will print more money to escape fiscal chaos, and Bitcoin is immune to inflation.
>
> The curse: a 5% yield on 10-year Treasuries has quietly become DeFi's new benchmark. Higher "risk free" Treasuries pulls capital toward safer returns, making crypto-native yields look riskier and harder to justify.

*Teaches:* "blessing/curse" contrast structure inside an explainer — opener as a contained thesis, body as parallel paragraphs.

### Exemplar 1B — Crypto efficiency vs Visa (339c, chart)
> Crypto is one of the most efficient industries in the world. Ethereum's market cap per unit of operating expenses is significantly higher than some of the largest and most profitable Web3 tech companies. This is the power of blockchain technology.
>
> In 2023, Visa incurred $11.6 billion in operating expenses versus Ethereum's $100 million.

*Teaches:* Thesis-first opener, then a single killer comparison stat as the entire body. Punchier than a long unpack.

### Exemplar 1C — Global debt + Bitcoin hedge (457c, chart)
> This chart shows cumulative world debt ($ trillion) from 1957-2025.
>
> Global debt exploded from under $50 trillion in the late 1990s to over $300 trillion today.
>
> Our global financial system is addicted to leverage, and there's no credible path to reverse it without default, inflation, or financial repression.
>
> Bitcoin is a hedge against this debt spiral because it is a decentralized, non-sovereign, global, immutable digital asset with a hard cap supply.

*Teaches:* Stair-stepped explainer — each line escalates the stakes, ending with the asset implication. Classic macro-to-Bitcoin payoff.

### Exemplar 1D — Coinbase + Apple Pay onramp (579c, link)
> Coinbase is integrating Apple Pay into its fiat-to-crypto payments offering.
>
> The product, Coinbase Onramp, provides a one-click buy that reduces the number of hoops people have to jump through to fund their self-custody wallets.
>
> This is exactly how crypto will gain hundreds of millions of additional users. Imagine a world where Apple and Google integrate self-custody stablecoins into their wallets, enabling seamless transactions across platforms like Amazon, Uber, DoorDash, Shopify, Airbnb, and eBay.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* News fact → mechanic → "imagine a world where…" forward projection. The visionary close.

## 2. Crypto Use Case of the Week (Recurring Series)

**When to use:** roughly weekly. Real-world, often non-financial, deployment of blockchain — government records, payments, real estate, identity. The format itself is the argument: "look how many of these there are."

**Skeleton:** (1) label line `Crypto Use Case of the Week:`; (2) one-sentence wire-style headline naming the deployment; (3) optional 1-2 sentences of factual amplification; (4) source link.

**Length:** 170–500 chars. Tight. **Source:** almost always a link. **Variants:** "Bitcoin Reserve of the Week", "Crypto Institutional Adoption of the Week" — same shape, different label. **Signature moves:** geographic diversity is a feature; minimal editorializing; the format itself is the argument.

### Exemplar 2A — NJ tokenizes property deeds (303c, link)
> Crypto Use Case of the Week: New Jersey to Tokenize $240 Billion in Property Deeds in Landmark Real Estate Move. Bergen County will digitize 370,000 property deeds on the Avalanche blockchain in the nation's largest blockchain deed project.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* Label + wire-headline + one factual sentence + link. The compressed form.

### Exemplar 2B — Bolivia, USDT replacing pesos (376c, link)
> Crypto Use Case of the Week:
>
> In Bolivia Where Inflation is 25%, Crypto Is Starting to Replace Money.
>
> At the El Alto International Airport, a vendor lists prices of candy and sunglasses in USDT, and a top university pays its international faculty in Bitcoin.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* Label-then-break form, with a vivid concrete detail (vendor + sunglasses) as the proof.

### Exemplar 2C — XRP for US pharmacy payments (265c, link)
> Crypto Use Case of the Week:
>
> Thousands of US pharmacies can now settle prescription drug payments in cryptocurrency through a new blockchain-based system running on Ripple's XRP Ledger.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* The minimal version — label, one fact-dense sentence, link. No commentary needed.

### Exemplar 2D — Steak 'n Shake Bitcoin reserve (383c, link)
> Bitcoin Reserve of the Week:
>
> Fast food joint Steak 'n Shake, which has 393 locations in the U.S., made history by becoming the first major restaurant to launch a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve.
>
> The company announced that all payments received in Bitcoin will now be added to its Strategic Bitcoin Reserve.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* The "Bitcoin Reserve of the Week" variant — same skeleton, different recurring label.

## 3. News Headline + Allocator Takeaway

**When to use:** when a piece of TradFi/Bloomberg/WSJ/FT news genuinely matters for crypto allocators but the takeaway isn't obvious unless someone connects the dot.

**Skeleton:** (1) source-attributed headline as opener (`Bloomberg News:` / `Interesting WSJ article on…`); (2) 1-3 sentences re-framing what the news means for digital assets / allocators; (3) source link.

**Length:** 250–900 chars. **Source:** almost always a link. **Signature moves:** "Many assume X. This is only partly true." debunk; translates TradFi headline → crypto implication → sometimes the Amitis house view; sometimes ends with a "Welcome to [coined-phrase]" closer.

### Exemplar 3A — JPMorgan loans against luxury cars (425c, link)
> Bloomberg News: JPMorgan Lets Rich European Clients Borrow Against Luxury Cars
>
> Part of the promise of tokenization is that all assets, including luxury cars, real estate, art, invoices, and fund shares, can eventually be used as collateral. This will turn previously illiquid assets into productive financial assets.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* TradFi headline → "part of the promise of [X]" reframe → asset-class implication.

### Exemplar 3B — HFT speed war + DEX/MEV (851c, link)
> Interesting WSJ article on TradFi equity trading: High-speed traders are feuding over a way to save 3.2 billionths of a second
>
> Many assume that because crypto is decentralized, the "arms race" over speed disappears. This is only partly true.
>
> 80% of crypto trading occurs on centralized exchanges, and high-frequency crypto traders co-locate with matching engines just like in equities.
>
> But 20% of crypto trading occurs on decentralized exchanges, where high-frequency co-location doesn't chase nanoseconds. However, speed still matters in the "MEV game" because the race shifts to transaction ordering, where traders pay higher fees or use specialized infrastructure to get their transactions included first in a block.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* The "many assume X. Only partly true." debunk-and-explain structure. Long-form variant.

### Exemplar 3C — Walmart/Amazon stablecoins (387c, link)
> Walmart and Amazon Are Reportedly Considering Their Own Crypto Stablecoins.
>
> Why? Because accepting stablecoins as payments could save retailers the 2-3% interchange fees to credit card providers like Visa and Mastercard.
>
> Welcome to Stablecoin Summer!
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* Headline → "Why?" → mechanic → coined-phrase kicker. The "Welcome to X" closer.

## 4. Titled Trade Idea / Investment Thesis

**When to use:** for long-form, structured investment cases — usually pair trades, single-name theses, or scenario-ranged calls. Rare and high-signal; the format that most reads like a bank research note compressed onto X.

**Skeleton:** (1) bold-style title line (`Long X / Short Y Trade` or `[Asset]: The [Theme] Trade of [Year]?`); (2) setup of asset 1 (size, on-chain or fundamental data); (3) parallel setup of asset 2; (4) the economic/usage disconnect that justifies the trade; (5) trade construction; (6) what the structure isolates.

**Length:** 600–1,500 chars. **Source:** often a chart, rarely an outbound link — the post is the argument. **Signature moves:** post = one-page memo; parallel construction forces comparison; closes by explaining what the trade structure isolates and what it does NOT take a view on; scenario ranges (base/bull/structural rerating).

### Exemplar 4A — Long Solana / Short Cardano (1,257c, chart)
> Long Solana / Short Cardano Trade
>
> Cardano (ADA) is a Layer-1 blockchain with a ~$15 billion market cap, making it the 10th largest crypto asset by market value. Despite this scale, Cardano processed just 760,000 transactions in October 2025, a negligible level of on-chain activity.
>
> Solana is also a Layer-1 blockchain with ~$75 billion market cap, the 7th largest crypto asset. But Solana routinely processes 40–60 million transactions per day, translating to well over 1 billion transactions per month and reflecting real usage across DeFi, trading, payments, and consumer applications.
>
> This usage disconnect shows clearly in economics. Cardano generates roughly $5,000 per day in chain fees, or ~$1.8 million annually, while Solana generates approximately $350 million per year, nearly two orders of magnitude higher.
>
> Long Solana and short an equal dollar amount of Cardano represents a classic fundamental token pair trade. Over time, tokens backed by usage, revenue, and developers tend to win, and those without struggle to keep up.
>
> By structuring the trade in a market-neutral way, we isolate the relative fundamental divergence between the two networks, without taking a view on whether the broader crypto market moves sharply higher or lower.

*Teaches:* The full pair-trade memo skeleton — title, parallel asset setups, economic disconnect, trade construction, what-it-isolates close.

### Exemplar 4B — Ethereum: Infrastructure Trade of 2026 (660c)
> Ethereum: The Infrastructure Trade of 2026? Bullish thesis from Moonwalker:
>
> Ethereum is increasingly being priced less like a crypto token and more like financial infrastructure. If Bitcoin is a store of value, Ethereum is a settlement layer with yield. The difference matters because infrastructure assets:
> • compound on adoption
> • monetize throughput
> • and get re-rated once institutions are allowed to own them
>
> Moonwalker Scenario Ranges:
> • Base case (adoption continues, macro stabilizes): $4,000–$6,000
> • Bull case (ETF + tokenization traction, easing liquidity): $7,000–$10,000
> • Structural rerating case (ETH priced as infrastructure + yield): $12,000+

*Teaches:* The single-name thesis variant — title-as-question, attributed source, bullets for properties + scenario price ranges.

## 5. Numbered List Explainer (N Reasons / N Ways / N Items)

**When to use:** when the underlying argument is genuinely enumerable. Almost always borrowed-and-attributed from a source (Pantera, Tom Lee) or a framework (GICS).

**Skeleton:** (1) setup sentence framing the question or claim; (2) optional attribution; (3) numbered list with short item names + clause of explanation each; (4) optional closing sentence with implication.

**Length:** 400–1,000 chars. **Source:** often a link or chart; sometimes neither — the list is the content. **Signature moves:** explicitly numbered (not bulleted) when ordering matters; uses "Em-dash – clause" format for inline list-item explanation; final item sometimes flagged as most important.

### Exemplar 5A — Five most common LD benchmarks (996c)
> Selecting appropriate benchmarks is crucial when evaluating active investment managers.
>
> Managers prefer benchmarks they can consistently outperform as it boosts their perceived skill and value. That's why most avoid using Bitcoin as their benchmark, even though it is often the most relevant for digital assets. IBIT (Blackrock's BTC ETF) is an attractive, low cost, and low operational risk entry point for institutions seeking crypto beta exposure.
>
> The five most common benchmarks we see for Liquid Directional managers are:
>
> 1. CCi30 – the 30 largest tokens by market cap, excluding stablecoins
> 2. MVIS – MarketVector has over 100 digital assets indices, some equal-weighted
> 3. Bloomberg Galaxy Crypto Index – launched in 2018, it's a capped market cap weighted index
> 4. Bitwise 10 Crypto Index – BITW is the top-10 tokens by market cap, excluding stablecoins
> 5. S&P Global – S&P has ~15 crypto indices, including an "Ex-LargeCap Index" that excludes BTC and ETH

*Teaches:* Frame-then-list — two paragraphs of context, then a clean numbered enumeration with em-dash explanations.

### Exemplar 5B — Tom Lee on DAT premiums (803c)
> Tom Lee of Fundstrat/Bitmine offers six reasons why (good) DATs deserve to trade at a premium:
>
> 1. Capital Markets Arbitrage – DATs trading at a premium can issue equity and buy crypto at a discount to NAV, creating accretion for shareholders.
> 2. Lower Cost of Capital – Public crypto companies can borrow more cheaply than individuals, especially using convertibles or bonds, sometimes near 0%.
> 3. M&A Upside – DATs trading at a premium can use their stock to acquire lower-valued peers and increase per-share holdings.
> 4. Operating Leverage – DATs can build businesses within the crypto ecosystem (e.g., ETH services) that generate additional token accumulation.
> 5. Native Yield – DATs can stake assets (e.g., ETH) to earn on-chain rewards.
> 6. The sixth and most important driver: credible management.

*Teaches:* Attributed list. Final-item-as-most-important closer is a recurring move.

### Exemplar 5C — GICS sectors and crypto as the 12th (426c)
> The Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) divides the economy into 11 primary sectors:
>
> 1. Communication Services
> 2. Consumer Discretionary
> 3. Consumer Staples
> 4. Energy
> 5. Financials
> 6. Health Care
> 7. Industrials
> 8. Information Technology
> 9. Materials
> 10. Real Estate
> 11. Utilities
>
> Amitis believes crypto will eventually become recognized as a new, standalone 12th sector alongside these 11 traditional GICS sectors.

*Teaches:* Pure-list variant — frame, list, then a single closing sentence that lands the Amitis view.

## 6. Question Hook + Answer

**When to use:** when there's a debate worth raising openly — Layer 1 wars, ETF speculation, comparison games. The question is genuine, not rhetorical.

**Skeleton:** (1) one-line question opener (often the entire first line, ending in `?`); (2) setup of why the question matters / current state; (3) optional answer or "here's the case" body; (4) optional source link.

**Length:** 130–600 chars. **Source:** often a chart; sometimes a link to the post that prompted the question. **Signature moves:** question is short and provocative — no qualifiers; body is balanced ("here's the bull case, here's the bear case") more often than one-sided; sometimes the answer is intentionally left open.

### Exemplar 6A — SOL flipping ETH? (259c, chart)
> Will Solana ($80B MC) flip Ethereum ($300B)?
>
> SOL/ETH is at an all-time high and the race for blockchain Layer 1 dominance is in full swing. "ETH or SOL?" is one of the most debated topics in crypto fund managers circles today (and Solana has great momentum).

*Teaches:* Provocative one-line question, then market-state context, then a soft directional lean. No hard answer.

### Exemplar 6B — Web3 → Web4 explainer (465c, link)
> We haven't even finished building Web3 and we are hearing more and more about Web4. What is it?
>
> Web1: Read‑only internet
>
> Web2: Read-write internet (centralized social platforms, user-generated content, etc.)
>
> Web3: Read-write-own (decentralized with blockchain and cryptocurrencies)
>
> Web4: The emerging "symbiotic web" that integrates AI, peer-to-peer networks, IoT, and (sometimes) brain-computer interfaces
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* Question → progressive enumeration as the answer. The "What is it?" hook with a definitional ladder.

### Exemplar 6C — Berkshire vs all of crypto ex-BTC/ETH (271c, chart)
> Digital assets are a $2.5 trillion market cap today. BTC ($1.2T) and ETH ($350B) account for 70% of this, and the rest of the 10,000+ tokens are cumulatively worth just under $1T.
>
> Now, would you rather own Berkshire Hathaway ($900 billion) or all of crypto ex-BTC & ETH?

*Teaches:* Market-state setup → "would you rather" question as the kicker. Question lives at the end.

## 7. Chart/Data Lead with Implication

**When to use:** when a single chart or stat does most of the work. He narrates what the chart shows, then draws the implication for crypto allocation or macro positioning.

**Skeleton:** (1) "This chart shows…" or stat-led opener naming the data; (2) headline finding from the data (1-2 sentences); (3) why it matters — systemic / macro / market-structure read; (4) asset or positioning implication.

**Length:** 350–700 chars. **Source:** always a chart or specific data table; often no link. **Signature moves:** opens "This chart shows…" or "[Stat] is at an all-time high"; cleanly separates description from interpretation; closes with macro-to-asset implication ("the best expression of this view is…").

### Exemplar 7A — Job openings vs S&P 500 since ChatGPT (493c, chart)
> This chart shows U.S. job openings (red) and the S&P 500 (blue), with the vertical line marking the launch of ChatGPT in November 2022. Since that moment, the two have diverged sharply. Growth in market cap no longer requires growth in headcount. The old model, where rising companies added both employees and market cap, has flipped. In the era of LLMs and automation, productivity gains are decoupling from labor. Welcome to the next phase of global growth: efficiency through fewer workers.

*Teaches:* "This chart shows…" → divergence narration → systemic re-frame → "Welcome to…" kicker.

### Exemplar 7B — Most Important Chart in Finance (473c, chart)
> This has been called the "Most Important Chart in Finance" because it shows the exponential difficulty of recovering from large losses.
>
> For example, to recover from a 10% loss, an investor needs an 11% gain. To recover from an 80% loss, though, an investor needs a 400% gain (5x).
>
> This is particularly important in crypto because some managers were down 80% in the 2022 bear market. Even though they were up 100-200% in 2023, they are still significantly below their HWM!

*Teaches:* Naming the chart, walking through a worked example, then landing the manager-relevant implication.

### Exemplar 7C — IMF debt projection → asset implication (466c, link)
> The IMF warns that global government debt is on course to exceed 100% of GDP by 2029.
>
> A core tenet of Amitis's top-down macro view is that fiscal and monetary debasement is leading to the continued devaluation of fiat currencies. The best expression of this view is to be long assets that central banks cannot print, like gold, equities, Bitcoin, and crypto.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* Single stat → "core tenet of Amitis's view" → "best expression of this view is…" — the stock house-view payoff line.

### Exemplar 7D — Helium fundamentals vs price (1,485c, chart)
> There's a huge disconnect between fundamental value and price in digital assets, and Helium (HNT) is a great example. (h/t Vikara)
>
> HNT was down -76% in 2025 despite the fact that Helium's monthly revenue grew from $200k in January to $1.7m in December, their total mobile paid traffic grew nearly 6x YoY, average daily network users reached 1.2m with their mobile network subscribers growing nearly 300% to 462k.
>
> Helium is in the decentralized physical infrastructure (DePIN) sector, which promises to provide real utility to ecosystem participants. This includes blockchain solutions such as storage, cellular/mobile, rendering, and GPUs, for example.
>
> Helium is a decentralized, peer-to-peer wireless network that enables devices to connect to the internet using LoRaWAN (Long Range Wide Area Network) and 5G technologies. Individuals install Helium Hotspots, which act as wireless network nodes. These hotspots provide network coverage in exchange for earning HNT tokens. The network also supports the Helium Mobile (separate token MOBILE) cellular subnetwork. Helium migrated to the Solana network in April 2024.
>
> In essence, Helium has built a global wireless network via individuals running hotspots (nodes) with very little capex, given the hot spots are paid for and owned by individuals.
>
> While the network expansion has naturally slowed as node operators cover more area, user growth has exploded, something crypto has longed for (actual users of the blockchain solution!).

*Teaches:* The long-form data-led case study — disconnect thesis, fundamental data dump, sector framing, mechanism explanation, payoff line.

## 8. Resource / Read Recommendation

**When to use:** pointing followers (often allocators or other managers) to a tool, dashboard, report, podcast, book, or article worth their time.

**Skeleton:** (1) name the resource and what it is; (2) one or two sentences on why it's valuable / what stood out; (3) direct link.

**Length:** 150–400 chars. **Source:** always a link. **Signature moves:** "is excellent" / "is a great resource" / "is a must read" / "is a great read" — recurring trigger phrases; calls out the specific section or data point worth landing on, not just the URL; sometimes notes who Amitis appears in or contributed to.

### Exemplar 8A — NilssonHedge for allocators (277c, link)
> NilssonHedge is a great resource for digital assets allocators. They aggregate hundreds of funds (with full transparency into the constituents) and produce indices for both Liquid Directional and Liquid Market Neutral strategies: [link]

*Teaches:* Name → what it is → why it matters for the audience → inline link. The compact resource-rec.

### Exemplar 8B — Electric Capital crypto market map (262c, link)
> Electric Capital's new crypto market map is excellent.
>
> They divide the 1,500 top projects across six main categories (Apps, Protocols, Developer Tools, Interoperability, Scaling, and Core Infrastructure) and 137 sub-categories.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* Two-line variant — short praise opener, one-sentence factual unpack, link.

### Exemplar 8C — Arca's Crypto Investing Paradox (309c, link)
> Arca's latest blog on the Crypto Investing Paradox is a must read.
>
> Skip down halfway to the "Crypto Investing Paradox" section that highlights the disconnect between the advanced capabilities of blockchain technology and the limited interest in using it.
>
> [link]

*Teaches:* "Must read" + reading instruction ("skip down halfway to…") — actively curating the user's path through the resource.

### Exemplar 8D — a16z State of Crypto 2025 (292c, link)
> Andreessen Horowitz's latest State of Crypto 2025 is a great read.
> [link]
>
> Interesting to see that Ethereum maintains its pole position as the L1 blockchain with the most developer activity, but Base and Solana are hot on its heels.

*Teaches:* Recommend → link → one specific datapoint Chris pulled out himself. Adds curatorial value beyond just sharing.
